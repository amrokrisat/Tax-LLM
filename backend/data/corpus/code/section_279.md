Section 279 addresses corporate acquisition indebtedness and can limit deductions on certain obligations issued in connection with acquiring stock or assets. Although it is not the first authority raised in every acquisition financing conversation, it is a useful overlay where the structure includes acquisition debt with equity-like features or other instruments that may blur the line between straightforward leverage and acquisition paper with special tax constraints.

For a transactional tax agent, Section 279 matters because financing analysis should not stop at Section 163(j). Where the parties are comparing alternative debt placements or considering seller paper, preferred instruments, or subordinated financing, Section 279 helps identify whether some debt-cost assumptions need to be discounted or modeled more carefully.
